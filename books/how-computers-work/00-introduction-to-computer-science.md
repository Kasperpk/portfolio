# Introduction to Computer Science

Computer science is according to *MTU* the "study of computers and computational systems". In the early era of computers anyone could gain an understanding of the internals of how the machinery worked. With more modern computing systems that is no longer the case and as a consequence computer science has become a very specialized with niches that studies a part of the whole field. There are specializations such as
* Algorithms and data structures 
* Software engineering
* Computer architecture
* Aritifical intelligence and machine learning
* Cyber security and networking
* Human-computer interaction

The goal of this work as a whole is to study the fundamentals and go down the path of mastery and discovery of beauty. We will cover how the theoretical idea of digital systems and computing and how this idea was realized and later applied. 

If you are one of those who read these paragraphs you most likely have alot of experience with digital tools yourself and work with them professional or personally. For most people using a computer means turning it on, running a few programs, clicking around and then shutting it down again at the end of the work day. This may be satisfactory for the majority of people and enough to accomplish their jobs. Other people are professionals for whom the majority of the value they produce is through this tool. "In this respect the programmer does not differ from any other craftsman: unless he loves his tools it is highly improbable that he will ever create something of superior quality" this phrase here is from Edsger W. Dijkstra meditations on advanced programming from  1962. Perhaps not everyone is a serious programmer like Edsger, an important figure in computer science i am going to return to, but even with a semi-technical role or standard office job you are left with a good five plus hours everday with this machine as it has become so embedded into work systems and our personal life. I believe Edsger's message is very important. To really produce the best work you can, you need to enjoy the tools you have and from my point of view that means understanding it's ins and outs. I imagine that professional drivers  would enjoy and perform better if they understand how cars accelerate, break, steer and so on and that a chef with a deep understanding of kitchen appliances and what happens when you mix certain flavors and food enjoys cooking even more. In the coming pages we're going to embark on a voyage of breaking down computing systems into it's elements one layer of abstraction at a time as we try to regain some craftsmanship. 

The goal is therefore to become a more sophisticated user of the machine itself and to find answers to interesting questions such as those presented in David A. Patterson and John L Hennesy of *Computer Organization and Design*.
* How are programs written in high-level language translated into hardware and how does the hardware perform these operations?
* What is the interface between software and the hardware, and how does software instruct the hardware to perform needed functions? 
* What determines the performance of a program, and how can a programmer improve performance?
* What techniques can be used by hardware designers to improve performance?

The authors write "without understanding the answers to these questions, improving the performance of programs on modern computers or evaluating what features might make one computer better than another for a particular application will be a complex process of trial and error, rather than a scientific procedure driven by insights and analysis." (page 9) The authors also remark that digital systems are continuously developing. For example programmers of the 1960s and 1970s had to deal with the primary constraint of limited computer memory whereas "programmers now need to understand issues that have replaced the simple memory model of the 1960s: the parallel nature of processes and the hierarchies of memories" (page 8). Understanding where we come from allows us to better gauge where we are heading, which can be important for anyone with a technical role.
