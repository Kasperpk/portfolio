There is a deep linkage between mathematics and programming and the ability to solve problems in general. Both authors makes an important opening point which is how we approach solving problems. Zeitz uses the mountaineering analogy while George makes some general proposition about questions we should asks ourselves when encountering a challenge. The mountaineering analogy uses the description of how someone might figure out how to climb to the peek of a mountain to explain these concepts that George state as a list of interrigoative questions which upfront should help **define** the problem. Inspired by mathematical problems George ask in the open, what is the unknown? what are the data? what is the condition? That means we are curious and investigating things relevant to the situation at hand. In the mountaneering analogy Zeitz describe how the person might go to nearby peaks, observe the surroundings and how the environment changes and behaves. The crux is to observe, investigate and be curiously asking questions before making the tactical and tool considerations. George follows up with a series of questions including *Is it possibly to satisfy the condition?* *Is the condition sufficient to determine the unknown?*  *is it insufficient?* *redundant?* *contradictory?* 

To climb a mountain one may investigate by moving to several nearby peaks in order to observe and spot the terrain. This investigation may lead to development of *strategies* such as leaving early in the morning to avoid the snow becoming soggy and using *tactics* such as crossing the river using a raft. Paul explains that there are three levels of problem solving, the strategy, the tactics and the tools. The tool level may in this analogy be to cross the river "it may require the party to strip from the waist down and hold hands for balance". Understanding and investigating the problem helps develop the strategy which in turn guide the other levels. When solving a problem like climbing a mountain, some exercises may be easy while other require overcoming great obstacles and negotiating these require **crux** and creative moves.

**Mindset when approaching challenges**
In his book *open to think* Dan Pontefract describes an experiment that he used to do with a group of students. The experiment is separating a class into five teams and the only instruction he gives them are "Go to the room you have been assigned to, read the instructions found in the envelope on the table, and complete your task" (Dan Pontefract p. 49-50). No further rules were declared. The teams should construct a tower with some specific dimensions using paper and straws. The crux of this problem was that Dan had left our some critical things at each team and the only way to complete the task was to coorporate with the other teams. What happened in most cases where that the teams became dictatorial, paniced and hopeless. A few students realized that "their thinking had to change" (Dan Pontefract, p. 52). They realized that nowhere it was stated that you couldn't leave the room and corporate with the other teams. The teams had to step out of the stress and pressures of competition and think openly. We've come so accustomed to think that any such activity has to be competitive even without the instructions saying anything about competition and a prize. 

People may consider outsourcing their thinking to tools such as *artificial intelligence tools* including Siri and Alexa. The chance is that you become an indifferent thinker, lazy and not actively seeking to dream, decide and do. Dan also opens up his book with the Robert Frost poem *the road less traveled by*. In this poem the narrator is standing at a crossroad and while most people assume that it's about taking the road less traveled, Dan explains that it is a description of someone who is open to think as he observe multiple roads and decide the one which to go and commit to it. It's shifting between the creative, critical and applied thinking.  Open to think is  about "freedom to think, the ability to assess, and the nurturing of creativity. It requires patience, judgment, collaboration and action." "Leaders ought to be striving for an environment where imagination is encouraged and critiquing leads to better decision-making, while thoughtful execution delivers postiive and sustainable results." (p.20-21, Dan Pontefract). If we are short term trying to race to complete tasks it work against our ability to produce value in the long term. Using the words of Dan it may indeed be time to "rethink how we are thinking". The advise to slow down and think for ourselves instead of letting other people opinion and our own existing biases and emotions control our actions is probably the most important advise here and for solving problems effectively.

But is there a better way of writing this algorithm? Well we know that all numbers that are lower than the highest of the numbers wont work.
