The brilliant thing about the use of logical operators is that they are simple. They only accept true/false inputs and the output is also only true and false. Unlike ordinary arithmetic, Boolean algebra operates over a discrete domain: 0 and 1. This discreteness is precisely what makes it physically realizable. Electrical systems naturally exhibit binary behavior — a switch is open or closed, a voltage is high or low, a transistor conducts or blocks current. To realize this we have to go to the bottom layer of abstraction which is a introduction to *physics* and particularly the natural force *electricity*. To fully grasp this force we need to center in on an individual atom consisting of a central core, the nucleus that holds protons and neutrons. These particles can be charged, in fact the protons and electrons are said to be charged, where electrons have a negative charge and protons are positively charged.

All matter consists of atoms, and the differences between materials stem from the unique ways their atoms are composed and arranged. Wood, copper, and stone, for example, can be distinguished at the atomic level because each has its own specific combination of protons, neutrons, and electrons. A carbon atom, for instance, contains 6 protons and 6 neutrons in its nucleus, with 6 electrons orbiting around it. Carbon is considered an _element_ because it is made up entirely of carbon atoms. In contrast, some substances, such as water, are _compounds_ or molecules — they contain more than one type of atom, in this case hydrogen and oxygen.

One interesting property of certain materials, like copper, is that they have a single free electron in their outermost shell. These free electrons can move easily from one atom to another, which makes such materials good conductors of electricity. Metals are typically excellent conductors, while materials like plastic, rubber, and wood are _insulators_, meaning they do not allow electrons to move freely. This is why copper is commonly used to make electrical wires, while rubber is used as an insulating coating to keep us safe from electric shocks.

You may have experienced a small snap when touching a metal door handle or noticed that a balloon can stick to your hair or a wall. This is caused by the buildup of _static electricity_. Friction — for example, between a balloon and your hair — transfers some electrons from one object to another. Because opposite charges attract, your hair is pulled toward the balloon, and the balloon can cling to surfaces without falling. In this case, the electric attraction is stronger than the pull of gravity.

The electricity that powers our devices, however, is not static but _electric current_ — a continuous flow of electrons generated by sources such as batteries or power plants. The amount of power produced is typically measured in _kilowatts_ and reflects the rate at which electrical energy flows from the positive to the negative terminal.

When you plug a vacuum cleaner into a wall socket, the plug’s two pins connect to wires that allow electricity to flow in from the supply and then back out again, driven by the potential difference maintained at the power source. It’s a process so routine that we often forget how remarkable it truly is — a constant, invisible stream of charged particles powering the modern world. # From physics to devices
As electricity began to get widespread recognition in the mid to late 18th century, on the back end of discoveries of pioneers like Benjamin Franklin. A rigorous scientific effort began to study it in greater depth and in 1800, Alessandro Volta created the _voltaic pile_, one of the first true batteries. Later, James Clerk Maxwell formulated the mathematical link between electricity and magnetism, and Georg Ohm described the precise relationships between voltage, current, and resistance in electrical circuits. By the late 19th century, electricity had shifted from a scientific curiosity to an essential force in modern life. Electric light bulbs, radios, telegraphs, and motors revolutionized industry and transformed everyday living.
Amid this progress, a few key observations hinted at entirely new possibilities. In 1886, philosopher and logician Charles Sanders Peirce wrote to his former student Allan Marquand, noting that “logical operations could be carried out by electrical switching circuits.” In that letter, Peirce even sketched a design for a machine built from “16 electromagnetic elements” capable of executing logical processes.
![[Charles Pierce sketch of logic machine.png|center]]
Figure 1.4 Charles Sanders Pierce sketch of logic machine in his letter to former student Allan Marquand. 

Decades later, Peirce’s insight would help inspire a 21-year-old MIT student named Claude Shannon. In 1937, Shannon completed his groundbreaking master’s thesis, _Symbolic Analysis of Relay and Switching Circuits_. At the time, computing was still largely analog or manual — laborious and prone to human error, especially when looking up large calculation tables or performing complex computations by hand. Shannon showed that electronic circuits could be systematically designed to carry out **Boolean logic** — the algebra of true and false — using relays as mechanical switches. In his framework, “true” and “false” could be represented by high and low voltages, allowing circuits to execute logical operations. His thesis demonstrated how such designs could even perform arithmetic tasks like addition, subtraction, and multiplication. This  connection between electronic circuits and mathematical logic is the idea of which our digital computers rely. That Shannon was the only one to make that connection back then is likely not the case but his master thesis has been considered the most important of the century marking a turning point for digital computing.
## The switch that changed the world
 The LED that lights up in figure 1.2 in essence represent one bit of digital information, an electronic signal that is either on of off. In order to produce the kind of logic that Claude Shannon's presented in his master thesis, the electronic systems needs a mechanisms for switching on and off the electrical signals. Shannon used a mechanical mechanisms called a relay and this device has since have gone through different eras. The relay was originally designed for telegraphs, where it was mechanically flipping the magnetic arm in order to represent a dot or a dash, signs known from morse code. When the sending telegrapher pinned down the arm of the handle, electric current began to flow towards the receiving end cause a magnetic switch to flip up by the use of electromagnetic force. Now this device while revolutionary, ran into a few problems among them, those discussed by Richard Feynman in his 1959 talk. A single bit of information leaves a lot to be desired and to really put digital systems to work a lot of switches are needed to perform operations like arithmetic. The relay is sizeable in nature and had to effect that any useful computer had to be as well. Perhaps more challenging was that these electromechanical mechanisms broke down quite often as the metal broke. Speed of operations were also limited because for a mechanical arm to finish it's move just takes time. If digital computers really had to unleash it's potential something more durable had to be invented.  A new device was getting popular in electronic circuits perhaps did not do much to the size, but instead in speed and reliability. This was the vacuum tube and it was already being used in devices such as radio design to amplify both telephone and radio signals. The ENIAC computer took advantage of this technology and in its entirety used more than 17.000 vacuum tubes.

This operator works on only one input and inverts it from true to false and vice versa. The NMOS transistor behaves according to standard logic, when high voltage is applied the circuit is closed and current is allowed to pass from each terminal, towards the output. The PMOS transistor is designed to work in the opposite way, such that when high voltage is applied, the circuit is open thus no current runs from terminal to terminal. If high voltage is applied to the NMOS transistor, the circuits becomes closed and since it is connected to ground or 0 volt, the output flows towards that direction and the output loses any voltage. If low voltage is applyied to the input, the NMOS transistor circuit opens as is standard logic, but the PMOS closes and by design it is connected to high voltage running from the top at V<sub>dd</sub> and the high voltage signal propagate to the output signal.
