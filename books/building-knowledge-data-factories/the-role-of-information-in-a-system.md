Decision theory offers an explanation for why people and organizations value data, as it improves their *utility* through more accurate view of the world as it is. I like to use another perspective to complement the idea of utility and decision problems. In her influential book _Thinking in Systems_, Donella Meadows references a Dutch experiment. Two groups of households were fitted with electric meters. In some homes, the meters were installed in the basement. In others, they were installed in the front hall. When the experiment ended the results were quite substantial. The difference in energy consumption between the two groups was nearly 30 percent. Meadows use this experiment to illustrate how systems work and what can happen when a system receives **feedback**. In this case the feedback was about the energy consumption and the mechanisms with which this made an impact is through what she calls a **feedback loop** that now was a part of the household system. 

For the families with meters in the hall, every trip past the device was a feedback event — which the system now had access to enabling it to behave differently toward the goal such as energy conservation. The families with basement meters never received that real-time feedback. The information existed, but it was buried out of sight. Without visibility, there was no loop, and without a loop, no change.

This illustrates the importance of having information in any system and also how accessible it is. I like to highlight two quotes from her book which carries a lot of weight for anyone working with data. 

> **“Missing information flows is one of the most common causes of system malfunctioning.”**  
> (_Thinking in Systems_, p. 157)

And that

> **“Thou shall not distort, delay, or withhold information (in a system).”**

Meadows define a systems "as the collection of elements, interconnections and purpose" and this definition is broad enough to describe anything from Dutch households, football teams, data teams, trees or entire businesses. Systems thinking and utility of decisions theory offer two complementary explanations for why and how data is valuable. Agents that navigate dynamic environments with imperfect information find more optimal solutions to decision problems. Secondly systems are enabled to optimize behavior towards more desirable outcomes and *learn* through feedback loop mechanisms that comes with information. 

We may use this premise to explain why organizations devote significant resources to cloud storage, computing power, and teams of engineers and analysts. It must be the hidden costs of not having timely, accurate information that causes management to make those decisions. There are many such applications as well in our everyday lives which is why we probably quite intuitively accept the "data-driven" sentiment. When we drive cars we would feel quite blind without the dashboard displaying data about speed, fuel, and location. As we drive, we glance at the information, and each glance is feedback allowing us to adjust our behavior to arrive safely at the right location while avoiding a speeding ticket.

Intervening in information flows is among the most effective things you can do to a system. Delays. Meadows ranks delays at #10 — meaningful but lower leverage than information flows. Data latency is a delay in
   a feedback loop. When a business receives last month's revenue figures to make this week's decisions, the feedback
  loop has a structural delay baked in. The system cannot respond to present conditions — only to past ones. This is not
   a dashboard problem. It is a systems architecture problem, and it has a formal name.

  Information flows (#6). When a data team builds a new data product — a real-time operational metric, a unified
  customer view, a model that surfaces risk early — they are not building a report. They are restructuring the feedback
  architecture of the organization. This is a leverage point intervention. That framing elevates the work and explains
  why data infrastructure decisions have strategic consequences that extend far beyond IT.
