## Analytical maturity
The role of data and analytics within companies will continuously change and companies have to find it's role in their specific organization. The data-driven operating models we have studied so far all make some comment about culture or team enablement. Bill Schmarzo is likewise highlighting this in his big data business maturity index where organizational culture can become a bottleneck for succeeding with data. I believe most businesses stand Infront of a change management task where they have to bring experimentation and data literacy together into the workforce. This effort may lead to more enterprise-wide adoption and activation of data. We may see figures like figure 5.1 which is inspired by Bill Schmarzo's work. There may be different things to handle for the aspiring company reaching for what Schmarzo calls the metamorphosis stage. The data-driven operating model addresses some of these obstacles by realizing that companies must orient their data work towards the business by defining requirements and practicing data management. Then comes the data infrastructure that is the hardware and software such as data warehouse and lake house technologies to avoid the spider-web architecture.

What the drawing also shows is that data culture is sitting there in the middle as something companies need to handle before reaching higher analytical maturity levels. In Carl Anderson's book *Creating a Data-Driven Organization: Practical Advice from the Trenches* put words to the graph writing that organizations need prerequisites like technology to collect, store and transform data, which as shown in the figure 5.1 as software and hardware capabilities. Collecting data is not enough. In order to drive organizations with data, that data has to be of the right quality and *fit for purpose* thus we need a bond between what data we collect and what we need in the business. A third component involves the skills and capabilities of workers who can "asks the right questions of the data, who have the skills to extract the right data and metrics and use that to inform next steps". The maturity index also often have a component that is about the types of analytics that the business uses. The problem with viewing analytics as a hierarchy is that we might downgrade certain types which may be viewed less fancy. Anderson addresses this misunderstand and writes that analytically mature companies do not replace or leave reports and dashboards, but instead open up their catalog of analytics to also include that which is considered more sophisticated. Most organizations maybe recognize themselves somewhere on this continuum and it may even be that within the organization there exists teams with varying degrees.  
## Cultures that progress analytical maturity
Similar to the data-driven operating models, the maturity index imply that there are some key components that needs to fit together to create the foundations for data success. Those are for example

1) The appropriate technologies that supports the analytical ambitions which often include data warehouse or lakehouse technologies as well as IT-landscapes to support the operations but also the data requirements of the business. This is often the job of enterprise, IT and data architects. 
2) Good processes in place to define business initiatives and align data projects with the right use-cases. 
3) Training and recruiting the right personnel that asks the right questions and understand how to work with data. 

Carl Anderson summarizes this very well in his definition of **data-drivenness** as "building tools, abilities, and, most crucially, a *culture* that acts on data". It is the fourth point that this section is going to be about, as it is a key component (perhaps most crucial) of succeeding with data. There is a great amount of work to be done for those companies that want to reach metamorphosis and live up to the standards which Anderson call the *hallmarks of data-drivenness*. Anderson's hallmarks has a lot to do with organizational culture. He devotes a dedicated chapter to culture a theme that runs throughout his book. Culture is perhaps the most important ingredient when it comes to forming the data-driven organization because as data moves through the knowledge and data-centric factory culture shapes what goes in and comes out on the other end. When we build data into the systems of the organization the strength comes from the quality of the data which also means the richness of the context. The more high quality data that we have access to, the more nuanced and valuable it becomes allowing to make decisions at very granular and detailed levels. It's therefore a nice trait to have an organization where sharing is rewarded. Share the data, information and knowledge so that each member of the organization have the richest possible context to work from. Anderson opens up the chapter with a quote from Satya Nadella, the CEO at Microsoft 

> A data culture isn't just about deploying technology alone, it's about changing culture so that every organization, every team and every individua is empowered to do great things because of the data at their fingertips. (Anderson, p. 285)

This is hard work because it won't happen by itself. The default is often data hoarding as Anderson names it and this will be pulling the organization without proactive efforts to prevent it. Data and business professionals need to work against this pull by working on increasing the data quality and trust that sharing data will not be used against them. For example by sharing data to people on the "floor" they can adjust their behavior directly through the feedback loop instead of it going through multiple steps such as an analyst or middle manager.  

In addition to sharing information and data Anderson brings up a second hallmark which aligns with the message i introduced earlier about data literacy. Matthew Benham's impact at FC Midtjylland is hard to overlook. He is if anyone a great example of what it means to be data literate. Broad data literacy is the second cultural characteristics and to me that makes perfect sense. I spoke about how data literacy can be compared to acquiring a second language that requires immersion in the target language. Teams and individuals throughout the organization have a greater chance of succeeding and using data productively if they understand this language, have the right skills and prefer data as evidence for what they accept as knowledge. Being data literate means managers will prioritizing training their staff, have visions for data usage, understand the importance of investing in data and infrastructure capabilities as well as use data in their own strategic work. 

Moving on to the third characteristic Anderson calls i "Goal-First Culture". As the data literate organization value evidence based decision and acting on data it means people start to ask "What kind of data or information do we need as evidence or feedback?" That means the the first thing is to define what to collect and measure on. Matthew Benham did this by beginning to collect raw player statistics on which he relied decisions about recruitment and player abilities. Anderson also offers an example about one company that wanted to decide on what location to open up their new office. They would score a set of alternatives based based on data such as "Gallup's Well-being index, talent pool, cost of living, number and costs of flights to New York, etc.-". They decided that these sets of information and with all this context giving some weights, then the best office location could be chosen. We could also imagine a choice such as which car to buy. Using data to inform you, you would then need to define the goal such as finding the best car for the price. There might be several features that need to be weighted according to your preference such as price, size, color, brand and so on. Defining the goal first makes it clearer what to look for and how to adapt the behavior in response to the feedback that the information provides. Anderson describes how top level KPIs or strategies "cascade down to business units that then may set their own dependent sub KPIs, which may flow down to lower-level metrics and targets" (Anderson, p. 293). 

The fourth and fifth characteristics are to me some of the most interesting and important ones when it comes to organizational culture and style. The fourth characteristic is about *curiosity* and *inquisitee* two words i like a lot. These to qualities when adopted means infusing scientific qualities into the blood of the organization. Scientists are continuously testing and experimenting and in recent years some of the most growing companies are known to run a very high number of experiments every single day. The scientist or growth mindset is to do these experiments with an  attitude that does not take rather wins or failures personally. A scientific paper showcase this by using a "passive voice" (Anderson, p. 295). It's important that we break free of taking things personal which allows us to challenge the status quo and opinions of others. It also makes it less difficult to admit ignorance. The fifth characteristic Anderson calls iterative, learning culture and that is strongly connected to the growth or scientists mindset explained above. Mistakes are to be embraced maybe we can go so far to say savored. Closely connected with continuous experimentation and testing, iteration means our approach is to prototype, design, testing is a continuous loop. This is exactly the design thinking principles that should run the development process. 

Alas we've come to the last hallmark of Anderson's culture for data-drivenness. In order to bring the puzzle pieces together there must exists some sponsorship and leadership commitment to increasing the data literacy and data-drivenness of the company. Emerging out of the data management discipline is new C-level roles such as the chief data officer (CDO) or chief technology officer (CTO). The CDO role is a dedicated resource who ultimately has the mandate to guide the organizational use of data. Being somewhat removed from day-to-day operational tasks the CDO can look for new opportunities to use data and think strategically about the way data is used. The CDO role also play an important part in shaping the culture through leading by example. By showing examples and communicating how data is used the CDO takes a leading role in building the data-drivenness culture. Building the data foundation and knowledge factory also cost money and so the leaders must accept this trade-off and understand what the pros and cons are. Leaders must know all the puzzle pieces, including their own role in succeeding with data. 

As a final word, there is no doubt that success with data requires a lot of hard work. There is not way around that. Nothing worth while comes easy and that is true for analytical mastery as well. Building cultures and capabilities that constantly evolve requires constant development and attention but the benefits can be there when done right.
