All of the DDOM's i introduced in the previous section included some element of discovering and defining requirements or use-cases as a key component for succeeding with data. This means  collecting data in of itself does not produce value or make companies data-driven. It's when we can understand the entire systems, what are the important stocks and how to create feedback loops that are meaningful and useful. The use-cases and requirements insights activities aims spend time with these ideas and there are tools and techniques we can use to make this activity more deliberate, formal and structured. 

The perhaps most cited or book building data warehouse solutions by Ralph Kimball is the *data warehouse toolkit methodology*. This book was first published in the 1990s and has been considered a industry gem ever since. One of the reasons the book might have enjoyed so much success is that Kimball asks one to be oriented towards the business. His methodology includes a well structured and comprehensive requirements definition phase that precedes any implementation. His requirement definition involves close contact with people from all parts of the organization and uses scientific rigor to make sure we capture and understand the context and full set of demands from the people who are involved in business activities. 

Bill Inmon another popular figure in the area of data warehousing since the early days also favored this approach but questioned the success of defining requirements upfront. In his book *Building a data warehouse* which first came out in 1992 he reflect upon the difference between software development and data warehouse development. At this time it was common to use traditional waterfall methods for developing software applications and he argued that this approach of trying to define everything upfront in a series of interviews with stakeholders was not suited for data and analytics development. As he writes the requirements come from end user saying “*Give me what I say I want, then I can tell you what I really want*”. Recently this challenge that have been embraced, for example Bill Schmarzo's *thinking like a data scientist* methodology to building and designing analytical solutions. While it similarly draw and pull requirements from actual business objectives and challenges it incorporates ideas of **design thinking** principles. The design thinking method is consist of five phases. Emphasizing, ideation, prototyping, testing and feedback. Design thinking requires close collaboration with stakeholders as Kimballs methodology but acknowledge the caveat that Bill Inmon expressed in his pioneering work. It deals with it by getting prototypes in early on as it will allow users to discover there needs and provide feedback for another iteration of development. Design thinking also put heavy weight on the mindset and attitude that goes along the process. The most important is that we have an open-mind that rejects the status quo where being unreasonable is one of the mantras. Design thinkers rejects status quo and industry standards and instead adopt the attitude and mindset perhaps encapsulated very well in George Courus book titled *The Innovator's Mindset*. If we want better and new things we often have to look in a different direction than the current way of doing things. Courus writes "To create **new** and **better** ways of doing things, we need first to understand who we are creating them for." This is why design thinkers put heavy importance on emphasizing and understanding the customer as it is only be that deep understanding we can create products which really do well. I would say that combining the time-tested foundational methods found in work by Ralph Kimball and the more contemporary design thinking ideas gives the exact framework and state of mind to build the best solutions.  
## Personification of the enterprise and it's systems
The very first phase of design thinking *emphasizing* goes hand in hand with Kimballs message of orientation towards the business. To emphasize means stepping in to the systems of the business where we want to understand it's entirety to build solutions that integrate without friction and produce value. In my research for this chapter i came across the concept of *personas* something that marketing teams and product designers have defined and used to describe profiles that could be interested in their product. Closer to the development of data and analytical solutions it's also been used by application programmers to describe how some hypothetical end-user will use some application, helping designing the human-interaction experience. For example "When the user logs in, they will see a drop down menu. Then they can click on the item they want to buy and then be sent directly to the checkout page". This thinking has also been used by marketing and product design teams in which they step into the shoes of the customer and try to develop stories of hypothetical customers. For example they may describe some customer profile "*Peter, 35 years of age, two kids and married*". The marketing team then uses this to better understand the 360 view of the customers, what their days may look like, what kind of challenges and desires they have and how that might motivate action such as buying products and services from the company. This in-depth research provides insights about the exact pain points and desires, the driving force behind any economic transaction. For example the team might add details to the persona such as "*When Peter comes home from work and he first has to pick up his kids at school. Then he needs to go home and cook dinner for the family. The kids wants his attention and he don't have time for shopping so our product will solve this by....*." This requires involvement aligns exactly with the emphasizing phase of the design thinking and i view it as a very productive way of defining requirements which is going to help identify use-cases, ideas and designing the first solution prototypes. While we as data professionals might not see ourselves as marketers or product designers we do share the fact that we have customers. The customers are the collection of systems that use data we produce in the knowledge and data-centric factory. This exercise is about stop guessing and to make an effort to understand these systems just as marketing teams want to avoid guessing what the customers want. 

We might throw it back to the Dutch experiment that i have been referencing extensively throughout this book. Putting the meters in the front hall is an example of good systems design and it's likely that this would have been more obvious if one had spend time putting ourselves in the shoes of the household and their requirements as a whole. When defining requirements following Kimball's words of orienting ourselves towards the business we can make productive sessions with colleagues questioning them about their role, pain points and what the important stocks they are monitoring using the emphasizing and personas to build the entire view of the systems.

### The ability to listen and question
As requirements collectors we are researchers and the ultimate goal is to listen, observe in an emphatic way. These are *soft* skills can be trained so we get much more out of the initial phases in the design thinking methodology. It's actually possible to listen at multiple levels of depth. There is a level of listening which is deeply invested. In this deeply involved state you might notice smells, sounds and expressive tones in a way you have never been before in a conversation. Every detail is important when we interview and observe potential *buyers* or *end users* of our work. This type of listening is also what creates trust and interesting conversations that builds interpersonal connection which is good for the long-term prospective of your solutions and chances of success. This is what it means to step into the shoes of the *customer* which means you are giving yourself the chance of delivering something they actually desire and that builds trust that you will take care of the challenges they have and that you can solve their problem. 

Conversations are really about taking turns questioning and listening which is an art in of itself. It's particularly how we frame questions that makes a great difference for the kinds of insights we can get out of a conversation. In one end we have questions that are framed to be answered with a simple response such as yes or no. At the other end we can frame questions more open-ended which can lead to more insightful and interesting responses. For example asking the questions “*If you had access to any information, what would it be?*” can give very different information to work with as opposed to the question “*do you think there is any lack in the current information you have?*”.  Another example question may be “*describe the situations where more information could have been beneficial, and what is the current impact of lack of information?*” Examples of open-ended framing could be to start with “can you describe..”. “how would you describe the..”  You impose parameters to narrow the focus “How do you perceive.. in comparison with…”.  Asking related questions to dig deeper like “Can you give specific examples of…” Asking for sources to support claims “Are there any sources that support this?.”  Instead of asking “did you like the movie?” you could ask “How did you feel when you came out of the movie theater?" My claim is that together active listening, critical thinking and mastering questions can turn collaborative sessions into crafting seriously innovative and transformative outcomes where data may play a central part. 

This form of inquiry is connected to critical thinking which can be described as a cognitive state that sets aside personal biases and instead is curiously open to new viewpoints and perspectives. The list of points here below are some of the things that characterize a critical thinker.

1. Drawing conclusions based on solid systematic logical reasoning backed by best available evidence and reasonable assumptions
2. Understanding one own potential biases, viewpoints and point of view, being honest about one's feelings and developing empathy for oneself
3. Developing empathy for the opposing or other viewpoints and arguments
4. Looks for evidence and asks for the credibility and validity of the sources and synthesizing the information from different sources 
5. Open-mindedness and curiosity with no strong bond to any particular viewpoint and mental flexibility. 
6. Reading between the lines, looking for what rest behind the surface

It takes courage to admit ignorance or that you struggle to understand particular concepts. It takes courage to question popular opinions and put your own ego to the side. But activating the critical thinker in you is immensely important even if it creates some temporary friction. If there are something we don't understand we try to insist on finding arguments that follow logical rules which may require asking follow-up questions.
