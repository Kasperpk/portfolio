All of the DDOM's i introduced in the previous section included finding requirements or use-cases as a key component for succeeding with data. Just having data is does produce value or make companies data-driven. It's when we understand the entire systems by which i mean the business and it's important stocks that we can capture and monitor. These may come to us intuitively after reading and understand the first parts of this book and i believe common sense can get us along way. With that said there are tools and techniques we can use to make this activity more deliberate, formal and structured. 

The perhaps most cited or book building data warehouse solutions by Ralph Kimball is the *data warehouse toolkit methodology*. Here Kimball asks one to orient themselves towards the business by asking questions using interviews and other qualitative research methods to bring requirements to the surface. That is requirements gathering involves close contact with people from all parts of the organization and requirements are pulled from the people who are involved in business activities. 

The data-driven operating model from the Boston Consulting Group indicates this way of top-down defining requirements by starting from the vision of the business at the top of the pyramid. Bill Inmon another popular figure in the area of data warehousing since the early days also favored this approach by questioned the success of defining requirements upfront. In his book *Building a data warehouse* which first came out in 1992 he reflect upon the difference between software development and data warehouse development. At this time it was common to use traditional waterfall methods for developing software applications and he argued that this approach of trying to define everything upfront in a series of interviews with stakeholders was not suited for data and analytics development. As he writes the requirements come from end user saying “Give me what I say I want, then I can tell you what I really want”. Recently this challenge have been embraced, for example Bill Schmarzo's *thinking like a data scientist* similarly draw requirements from business objectives and challenges but incorporates ideas of **design thinking**. Design thinking is the perfect paradigm which can be divided into five phases: emphasizing, ideation, prototyping, testing and feedback. While still using qualitative methods and a top-down approach we get quicker from idea to prototype which then produce feedback for another iteration of development. A second reason i really like design thinking is the attitude that is promoted as it approaches problem solving with an open-mind and skepticism of the status quo. Design thinkers rejects status quo and industry standards and instead adopt the attitude and mindset perhaps encapsulated very well in George Courus book titled *The Innovator's Mindset*. If we want better and new things we often have to look in a different direction than the current way of doing things. Courus writes "To create **new** and **better** ways of doing things, we need first to understand who we are creating them for." This is why design thinkers put heavy importance on emphasizing and understanding the customer as it is only be that deep understanding we can create products which really do well. I would say that combining the time-tested foundational methods found in work by Ralph Kimball and the more contemporary design thinking ideas gives the exact framework we need. 
## Personification of the enterprise and it's systems
A related concept inspired by product and marketing teams it the concept of *personas*. Marketing teams, application programmers and product design teams may be using personas to understand the user or customer. For application programmers it is a description of how some hypothetical end-user will use some application, helping designing the human-interaction experience. For example "When the user logs in, they will see a drop down menu. Then they can click on the item they want to buy and then be sent directly to the checkout page". This thinking has also been used by marketing and product design teams in which they step into the shoes of the customer and try to develop stories of hypothetical customers. For example they may describe some customer "Peter, 35 years of age, two kids and marries". Then the story describes what may his day look like, what kind of challenges and desires does he face helping to understand exact pain points and desires, the driving force behind any economic transaction. "When Peter comes home from work and he first has to pick up his kids at school. Then he needs to go home and cook dinner for the family. The kids wants his attention and he don't have time for shopping so our product will solve this by....." The concept of personas is something which is exactly aligned with the design thinking principles and similarly can be very useful for defining requirements but also designing the first solution prototypes. **The ability to listen, think and question**
There are particularly a set of "soft" skills which are extremely useful when it comes to defining requirements in the way described above. It cannot be understated how important the personal relationships are, not only for job satisfaction but also to the success of particularly initial phases where design thinking ideas dominate our approach. The first skill is listening, but not just regular listening. There are levels of listening which we may have never ever experienced. There is a level of listening which is deeply interested state and where everything is absorbed. In this deeply involved state you notice smells, sound and the expressive tone because every detail is important for you. It's the type of listening that creates interesting conversations and builds interpersonal connection something that is much needed for long-term organizational success, and also for our job as data professionals. It's the way emphasizing and stepping into the shoes of the "customer" which means you are giving yourself the chance of delivering something they actually desire and that builds trust that you will take care of the challenges they have and that you can solve their problem. 

As we are met with professional problems of all sorts we are going to get challenged on socially and technically. Critical thinking can be relieving and important. The concept of critical thinking can be described as a cognitive state where we try to free us from our own personal biases and rely on logical reasoning. In general we may sum what characterize critical thinking in the six bullet points below as the process of. 

1. Drawing conclusions based on solid systematic logical reasoning backed by best available evidence and reasonable assumptions
2. Understanding one own potential biases, viewpoints and point of view, being honest about one's feelings and developing empathy for oneself
3. Developing empathy for the opposing or other viewpoints and arguments
4. Looks for evidence and asks for the credibility and validity of the sources and synthesizing the information from different sources 
5. Open-mindedness and curiosity with no strong bond to any particular viewpoint and mental flexibility. 
6. Reading between the lines, looking for what rest behind the surface

It takes courage to admit ignorance or that you struggle to understand particular concepts. It takes courage to question popular opinions and put your own ego to the side. But activating your critical thinker can prove immensely important to you and your colleagues, even if it creates some temporary friction. If there are something we don't understand we insist on that arguments should follow logical rules which may require asking follow-up questions. That leads to the next skill, mastering questions. 

How we frame questions can make quite a difference for what kind of information and conversation we have. In one end we have questions that are framed to be answered with a simple response such as yes or no. At the other end we can frame questions more open-ended which can lead to more insightful and interesting responses. For example asking the questions “If you had access to any information, what would it be?” can give very different information to work with as opposed to the question “do you think there is any lack in the current information you have?” Another example question may be “Can you describe the situations where more information could have been beneficial, and what is the current impact of lack of information?” Examples of open-ended framing could be to start with “can you describe..”. “how would you describe the..”  You impose parameters to narrow the focus “How do you perceive.. in comparison with…”.  Asking related questions to dig deeper like “Can you give specific examples of…” Asking for sources to support claims “Are there any sources that support this?.”  Instead of asking “did you like the movie?” you could ask “How did you feel when you came out of the movie theater?" My claim is that together active listening, critical thinking and mastering questions can turn collaborative sessions into crafting seriously innovative and transformative outcomes where data may play a central part.
