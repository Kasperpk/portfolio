Remember the household that was able to reduce energy consumption by having access to data? We may say that they used data to manage and run their household. This makes a very natural passage to businesses that like households are systems that can improve their utility by having access to the right information. 
### Monitoring stocks and flows in the business
Zooming out and viewing businesses using the systems thinking lens we can identify the elements which are the human resources, buildings, equipment, materials, machinery and so on. In addition there are intangibles including brands, patents, accumulated knowledge, experience and expertise which are less obvious but exists. Together these elements are interconnected in visible ways such as physical locations, online communication which become the business processes of the organization. The interconnections are also connecting things in obscure, complex and hidden that required a trained eye to see. The final thing we need for a system is a purpose and from an economic perspective microeconomic theory suggest that businesses exists to maximize profits which they try to achieve through a vision, mission and strategic plans. Some systems are learning and adapting if they have access to information that provide feedback loops to which they can adjust their behaviors, exactly what the Dutch experiment showcased. 

But Donnella Meadows does not stop analyzing systems at the elements, interconnections and purpose level and this is actually where things get really interesting. When we go deeper into systems thinking Meadows describe how we can analyze the system through *stocks* and *flows*. She writes how "a stock is the foundation for any system. Stocks are the elements of the system that you can see, feel, count or measure at any given time". Now that naturally grabbed my attention because counting, measuring and monitoring is almost synonymous with data. In the household it was the stock of energy consumption that the they had access to. 

![](feedback%20loop.png)
Figure 2.1 Feedback loop mechanism

Figure 2.1 shows this mechanism in which a feedback loop occurs in a business by monitoring stocks and adjusting to reach desired levels. In a business the important stocks could also be energy consumptions often stemming from larger profit maximizing KPIs. Most businesses track their sales, expenses and profits through some applications which automate a lot of the work. The business may then as the household have access to feedback loops on some important stock which alerts and reminds on the effectiveness of their current behavior both in terms of day-to-day operations and future strategies. 

Meadows also introduces flows and these are part of the adjusting and learning. Perhaps even more interesting flows are what causes stocks to change over time. As Meadows write *"It is the fillings, purchases and sales, deposits and withdrawals, successes and failures. A stock then, is the present memory of the history of changing flows within the system"* (p. 18). For example if we measure sales and profit as a stock then the flows are the behaviors and mechanisms that influence the level of the stock. There are both inflows and outflows to a stock and the sales of a business is most likely a result of multiple things including marketing efforts. If sales are trending down it might indicate that we need to change the marketing mix and more generally the inflows and outflows trace back to fundamental business disciplines such as operations, marketing, human relations, management, finance and so on. The marketing department may focus on the stock of sales by monitoring their own set of stocks including customer satisfaction, retention and churn rates which provide more details about the success of their current behaviors. The human-resource department might monitor employee satisfaction and the operations the quality of production and so on. In that way the systems and subsystems of the business have their own important stocks to monitor where they use their specialized knowledge to adjust and learn. 

Specialization is natural response to the increase complexity of business systems that picked up speed around the industrial revolution. While it's fairly easy for a household to understand that to reduce the stock of energy consumption requires closing the windows, turning down the heat, using less hot water in business things are more complicated. Knowing what levers to adjust on and experiments to try out is helped by deep understand of businesses and specialization in certain disciplines. This leads to the crux of managing and running businesses on data that we want to be

> Capturing, collecting and measuring important stocks in the business systems so we can monitor them, use it as feedback and use our knowledge or predictive models to learn and adjust behaviors that through inflows and outflows move stocks to the desired level.

Meadows sums this up very well as she writes "systems thinkers view the collections of system stocks and see the mechanisms for regulating them via the flows" (p.  25). 

The concept of stocks and flows can be applied to different types of analytics. We can both use it as a *monitoring* tool that oversees the level of stock which we might call descriptive analytics and usually this is a dashboard with various graphs, KPIs and numbers that summarize current level and trend. Then there is analytics that try to explain and understand why a stock might be the level that it is at something we might call *diagnostic* analytics. To make data-driven diagnostic analytics is commonly done with statistical methods that assigns coefficients to certain features that we expect to be explanatory to some degree. Models can also be *predictive* and *prescriptive* which means the model also make predictions about outcomes or choose among alternative options using data. There are examples of this use of data for example enterprises such as Amazon or Netflix are using data about customers to create statistical models which handle recommendation of products. One of the challenges to succeed with this type of analytics requires more data and which might not be readily available for capture. I want to again highlight FC Midtjylland perhaps inspired by the popular Moneyball. They made a model from a theory about a causal model that predicts raw talent. This required more data and at a much more granular level than summary statistics. Bill Schmarzo discusses this type of analytics in his book *AI human edge* 
reflecting that the area of data science is like many other areas being infused with AI. Schmarzo introduces something he call entity propensity models (EPMs). The entity propensity model centers around entities or elements like athletes, stores, employees or customers. By collecting all possible data about those entities brings context that can help paint complete pictures which enables companies to make very nuanced and detailed analytics. This type of analytics is somewhat different than monitoring something i call the scientific use of data as it reminds me of the evidence and rigorous approach used in science where data and statistics is used to confirm or deny theories rather than human judgement alone. 
## The data-driven operating models
We've now established a base understanding of the role of data in organizations and how it can be used to manage and run businesses. To reiterate it can provide feedback on important stocks like sales, quality of batches, costs, customer satisfaction, energy consumption, service level and so on. Again think back to the household who monitor their energy consumption whenever you need a reminder. That information can help the system adopt more appropriate behaviors once we understand the flows that impact the stocks. Secondly it's about finding more evidence based cause and effects models which may allow improved predictive and prescriptive capabilities. Now integrating these ideas and capabilities into businesses is the next big challenge that face us. The data-driven operating model (DDOM) may guide us by showing that it is the interconnection or combination of different elements such as insights requirements, data management and team enablement. 


![](The%20data%20driven%20operating%20model.png)

The insights requirements is perhaps the most important starting point that define the information or data requirements that business systems have and that exactly surrounds themes we have surfaced so far. For example HR functions are interested in employee satisfaction and general data on the workforce such as diversity in gender, age and origins, so think stocks to monitor and causal models. The team enablement element highlights complementary capabilities which supports or empowers the use of data such as education, upskilling and cultural transformations. The third pillar is data management which is about making deliberate efforts to produce the best data products as possible by dealing with data quality issues and designing architectures to support data lifecycles and distribution of data. 

Boston Consulting Group (BCG) offers a similar model in their article on how businesses can start data transformation. Their take on it starts from the vision and use cases and this corresponds to the the insights requirement element in the DDOM. Then they move on to ask whether the team has necessary competencies and if use of data has been embedded into the culture and processes, similar to team enablement. The data management element in the DDOM is split into both data governance and data infrastructure in the model from BCG.
