Remember the household that was able to reduce energy consumption by having access to data? We may say that they used data to manage and run their household. This makes a very natural passage to businesses that like households are systems that can improve their utility by having access to the right information. 
### Monitoring stocks and flows in the business
Zooming out and viewing businesses using the systems thinking lens we can identify elements such as human resources, buildings, equipment, materials, machinery and so on. In addition there are intangibles including brands, patents, accumulated knowledge, experience and expertise which are less obvious but exists as elements within the complex business systems. Together these elements are interconnected in ways that make up the working subsystems within the business. For example a person might be using a machine to move some materials or a HR-employee is having a conversation with a potential candidate. The business processes are the behaviors of the firm which transform the inputs into the products and services that the business delivers. 

For the definition of the system to be complete we need there to be a purpose and economic theory suggest that businesses exists to maximize profits which they try to achieve through operating their business and reaching for new opportunities to satisfy some demand in the market. 

But Donnella Meadows does not stop analyzing systems at the elements, interconnections and purpose level and this is actually where things get really interesting. When we go deeper into systems thinking Meadows describe how we can analyze the structure of systems in more detail particularly through *stocks* and *flows*. She writes how "*a stock is the foundation for any system. Stocks are the elements of the system that you can see, feel, count or measure at any given time*". Now that naturally grabbed my attention because counting, measuring and monitoring is almost synonymous with metrics, KPIs words I've encountered frequently as a data professional. Going back to the household that was introduced in the Dutch experiment, it was the stock of energy consumption that the they with the meter was able to monitor. So what happens when we begin to monitor important stocks is that which also took place in the household experiment.

Figure 1.1 Feedback loop mechanism

Figure 1.1 shows this mechanism in which a feedback loop occurs by monitoring stocks and adjusting to reach desired levels. In a business the important stocks could also be energy consumptions often stemming from larger profit maximizing KPIs, which is the ultimate stock of all stocks inside the most businesses. It's the reason why managers are interested in tracking their sales, expenses and profits. The business may then as the household have access to feedback loops on some important stock which alerts and reminds on the effectiveness of their current behavior both in terms of day-to-day operations and future strategies. 

Meadows also introduces flows and these are part of the adjusting and learning. Perhaps even more interesting flows are what causes stocks to change over time. As Meadows write *"It is the fillings, purchases and sales, deposits and withdrawals, successes and failures. A stock then, is the present memory of the history of changing flows within the system"* (p. 18). For example if we measure sales and profit as a stock then the flows are the behaviors and mechanisms that influence the level of the stock. Perhaps a new marketing campaign causes the stock of sales to rise, but at the same time the costs creates an outflow of cash and profits. If the sum of those two forces is net positive, it creates more profit. Many important decisions within companies comes down to choosing between projects and trying to estimate if they will be a net positive on the bottom line an exercise that is often difficult. 

When a business monitor it's stock such as sales the feedback loop enables the system to adjust and learn. So for example if the sales are trending down it might indicate that we need to change the marketing mix. There are many levers that can be adjusted as inflows and outflows trace back to fundamental business disciplines such as operations, marketing, human relations, management, finance and so on. Each function area typically have their own metrics or stocks they compare their success against. For example the marketing department may naturally be interested in conversion rates, customer satisfaction, retention and churn rates and the overall stock of sales. These more specific measures provides deeper insights into the effectiveness of their efforts and behaviors in that department. The human-resource department might monitor employee satisfaction and the operations the quality of production and so on. In that way the systems and subsystems of the business have their own important stocks to monitor where they use their specialized knowledge to adjust and learn. 

Specialization is natural response to the increase complexity of business systems that picked up speed around the industrial revolution. While it's fairly easy for a household to understand that to reduce the stock of energy consumption requires closing the windows, turning down the heat, using less hot water in business things are more complicated. Knowing what levers to adjust on and experiments to try out is helped by deep understand of businesses and specialization in certain disciplines. This leads to the crux of managing and running businesses on data that we want to be

> Capturing, collecting and measuring important stocks in the business systems so we can monitor them, use it as feedback and use our knowledge or predictive models to learn and adjust behaviors that through inflows and outflows move stocks to the desired level.

Meadows sums this up very well as she writes "*systems thinkers view the collections of system stocks and see the mechanisms for regulating them via the flows*" (p.  25). 

## Ways of using data
When working with data and analytics it's common to distinguish between different types of analytics. Some might call it a hierarchy of analytics going from descriptive, diagnostic to predictive and prescriptive. I am not sure i cherish this way of calling it a hierarchy because that suggest something is superior to the other. Descriptive analytics typically refers to looking at the cumulative history of stocks, using that knowledge to make decisions and adjusting behaviors which control inflows/outflows of that stock. Diagnostic analytics dives into the *why* which can be an interesting analysis particularly when we want to explore which levers we can adjust in a scientific way instead of guessing, so it effectively complements the descriptive analysis rather than replaces it. Predictive analytics uses data and scientific methods to make some estimate about how the level of stock would look like in the future or what the outcome will be of some discrete variable. Prescriptive analytics tries to use data to analyze what will happen if we do certain behaviors, that is adjust levers that might be sources of inflow or outflow. Together these four types of using data complements each other rather than forming a hierarchy that replaces one another. 

We now have a better understanding of the role of data in organizations and to reiterate it can provide feedback on important stocks like sales, quality of batches, costs, customer satisfaction, energy consumption, service level and anything we have technology and imagination to collect data about. Whenever you need to remind yourself of this basic idea, think back to the household in the Dutch experiment who reduced their energy consumption substantially. That information can help the system adopt more appropriate behaviors once we understand the flows that impact the stocks. This data can be used to fuel different types of analytics, descriptive, diagnostic, predictive and prescriptive and they all complement each other. 

But how do we actually redeem the potential value of data and integrate information into business and work systems? It's the equivalent to finding out what to monitor (energy consumption), how to monitor it and how to present that information into systems that need it to adjust their behavior. That is the question we will dive into next.
## The data-driven operating models
As i was researching this topic i stumbled upon something called *the data-driven operating model* or DDOM for the short version. It's a model that captures elements that helps integrate data into the operation models of businesses which is the exact question i had in mind. This model explains how it's the interconnection or combination of insights requirements, data management and team enablement.

The first leg i will discuss is the insights requirements. To integrate data and information into our business we need some idea of what stocks are important to collect data about and the kinds of systems that will use it. To me this is a very interesting process that can be highly creative and innovative. For example HR functions and managers have requirements about information on employee satisfaction and about the workforce such as diversity in gender, age and origins. 

The second leg is the team enablement element. So it might not be enough to have well defined requirements, instead it's according to this model also necessary to have the right people and skills that can ask the right questions, build the right solutions and use the data productively. 

Thirdly there is the pillar *data management* which i will return to as i will for the two other legs above, but in essence it can be understood as deliberate efforts, guidelines and practices to produce the best data products as possible.

The analytics layer investigate how well the team is equipped with the necessary competencies and how to ensure the data is going to be embedded and integrated into the systems, culture and processes. Governance and infrastructure are also data management tasks and so the Boston Consulting Group has divided the data management across different boxes.
