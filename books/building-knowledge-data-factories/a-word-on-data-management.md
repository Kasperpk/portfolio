As we have seen the most data-driven operating models include some notion of data management. The professional discipline of data management emerged as a specialization as organizations wanted to take data more serious. It was in the 1980s that terms like data governance, metadata catalogs and enterprise data models was born. In 1988 DAMA the Data Management Association international was formed and it really cemented that the discipline was now professionalized. In the 1990s a related framework, the enterprise architecture Zachman Framework was formed. It wasn't until 2009 that the first version of the DAMA-DMBOK was published a book for data management professionals based on the experience of countless data management projects. While the introduction of cloud computing and storage created new challenges and possibilities the discipline today is still about taking data serious. Many of these changes prompted DAMA to publish a second version of the DMBOK in 2017. All of these challenges and solutions are about redeeming the value of data and such ideas we can box into data management.

In her book *Navigating the Labyrinth* (by the way a title i find very fitting for a book on the topic of data management) Laura Sebastian-Coleman offer the following definition of data management.    "*Data management is the development, execution, and supervision of plans, policies, programs and practices that deliver, control, protect and enhance the value of data and information assets throughout their lifecycle*" (page 15). She identifies three major activities which data management consist of. These are related to *data governance*, *data lifecycle management* and *fundamental* activities. Her book takes departure in the DAMA and DMBOK which in it's most recent version consist of eleven knowledge areas.

## Raw materials of high quality
Data quality sits as a foundational activity within the data management discipline. I've met the *garbage in, garbage out* message countless of times in books, presentations and talks about data. Personally it seems that it's a discipline i for quite a while had parked in a corner even though i met the aforementioned words again and again. For me personally i think it's because the message often came in as a aside or small remark. in a larger piece of work and so it became small in my world. This is a *tragic mistake* if you ask Bill Inmon one of the major figures in the world of data who where right there from the beginning in the 1980s. He is still active and recently contributed to the foreword of Prashanth Southekals *Data Quality - Empowering Businesses with Analytics and AI*. This book as the title suggest does not put data quality in a corner or leave it as a small side note but instead puts it in the center a place where it perhaps should have been at least in my mind all along. 

Intangible assets make up larger share of enterprise values that has gone from 17% in 1971 to 84% in 2018 for the S&P 500  (p. 57, Southekal). These values reflect the significant changes in makeup of businesses where digital transformations have been rolling out in every single industry as technologies like IoT, 5G, artificial intelligence, robotics, quantum computing, analytics and blockchain becomes integrated into business architectures and processes. All of these technologies produce or consume digital objects where data is the raw material and the reason some people call it the the new oil. If you're in that boat you have to understand the significance oil had on creating a new industrial revolution. Oil fueled rail, road and air traffic, large scale manufacturing and industrial processes producing substantial economic growth through increased productivity, technological advancement and new consumer products. Technologies fueled by data might be creating comparatively transformative changes why attentions is plenty around AI, machine learning and the technology that spit out final results. The challenge is that the attention is unequally divided because without proper raw materials and foundation the output will take a hit. That foundational activities like data quality is not practiced first is what Bill Inmon refers to as a "*tragic mistake*"  (p. 18, Southekal). 

In many other fields professionals know the importance of working with the best raw materials. Fore example the best cooks will go great lengths to find the highest quality raw ingredients. It's because they know those ingredients will produce the best results in terms of flavor, texture and nutritional value while reducing the need for artificial enhancements and a lot of intervention in the cooking process. Professional cooks are very picky about their ingredients and know the difference it makes. As a data professionals i don't think we get trained in identifying high-quality data as much as trained chefs do. If that's the case the cost of poor data quality might never really show up as the business never get to work with anything but the scrambles inside their source systems. In an attempt to raise the issue and put a number to it Prashanth refers to several studies which tries to estimate it. Southekal refers to a Harvard Business Review study which find that data quality standards are rarely met around, only 3% of the time to their estimate (Nagle, Redman and David 2017). IBM did a study in 2020 which estimate that American businesses lose $3.1 trillion annually from having poor data quality. (p. 57, Southekal). Similarly Experian made a global survey in 2019 and 95% of the surveyed companies reported negative impacts from lack of data quality in their organization. A large part also reported that lack of trust and ability to use data to meet strategic objectives essentially pointing back to inadequacies in data quality. There is also a very interesting article from 2022 by the computer science professor at Stanford Andrew Ng. He shows that data quality initiatives were the most effective in improving the predictive capability of an machine learning model as opposed to adjusting the model and it's parameters.

The most productive place to begin getting more value from data is likely at it's very origin and why data quality is a core pillar of data management. The call to action here is to balance our focus and realize that the quality of data might produce perhaps 80% of the value while 20% can be added in the processing in later stages. But that means we have to learn to identify good and bad data quality. 

**Data fit for purpose**
Can we get more specific about it means for data to be of quality? David Garvin, a Harvard Business School professor made a contribution back in his 1987 research article titled “Competing on the Eight Dimensions of Quality.” published in Harvard Business Review. In that article he proposed that quality of data can be understood through a set of dimensions. DAMA similarly have several aspects or dimensions that they measure data quality against where the following six are highlighted as the core ones

| Data quality dimension   | Meaning                                                                                                                |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Accuracy                 | The degree to which the record really resemble the actual event or entity in the physical world                        |
| Completeness             | The degree to which the record is described through a complete set of features to which the entire context is captured |
| Consistency              | The degree to which records within the same dataset follows have the same attributes, data types and structure         |
| Timeliness               | The degree to which the data is relevant in a time perspective                                                         |
| Uniqueness/Deduplication | The degree to which each record is uniquely generated and can be identified precisely                                  |
| Validity                 | The degree to which the data can be trusted in how it has been captured so as to minimize biases                       |

Accuracy, completeness, consistency and all of the above are examples of dimensions that capture some **intrinsic** qualities of the data. 

There are some equally important **extrinsic** aspects that "*data is of high quality when it is **fit for use** in operations, compliance and decision making*" and similarly Dr. Thomas Redman writes "*data is of high quality when it is fit for intended use in operations, decision making and planning*" (Redman, 2016). We have to think about the entire set of dimensions because having perfectly accurate and complete data matter little if data is not accessible and used productively. Data has to somehow take up a meaningful role and put to use in decision making and planning. Together these qualities make data *fit for purpose* and that is the goal of practicing data quality. 

**Data quality one step further**
The call to rebalance our focus towards quality of raw materials and making data fit for purpose is some of those ideas from data management that i hope to bring into the way i work with data. There are of efforts we can take to prevent, mitigate and tackle data quality issues. So let's first talk about how we can prevent data quality issues. First of all being aware of the importance of data quality is a very good starting point. That's what the first part of this section about data quality have convince me to be and perhaps the reader as well. The next thing we can do is using plenty of time upfront analyzing what kind of data is needed such than when we built or invest in systems that become our data foundation we have considered what will make it fit for purpose. If we are going to invest in new technology that becomes part of the IT-landscape that generates data, then data requirements as well as operational should help drive the decisions, which means analysts and business people should be part of the conversations early on. Many systems have manual data entry and measures to ensure data quality could be to built-in conditions to minimize errors for example by having data validation rules or dropdown menus making it easier to uphold consistency. For example a system might disallow writing text in a field which is supposed to be numeric or ensure that entry follow the right formats. It could also be that entry is not possible to fill out unless certain fields have been filled out increasing consistency in the data foundation.

In addition to preventive measures we can also tackle data quality by doing some explorative data analysis a typical activity for data scientists. Explorative data analysis which typically starts by investigate the data looking at the distribution and correlations of data. Then the analysis goes deeper and looks into the some of the problems that may surface in this initial investigation. There can be biases in the data which stemming from how the data is collected which may surface as we look for common problems like missing data, duplicates and consistency. 

Prasanth's book also presents a step-by-step procedure that help locate the origin of data quality issues. To begin with it's important to organize ideas, opinions in some form of open forum that let's people voice issues around data. That could be supported using tools like **affinity diagrams** which helps group and organize issues. That may lead to insights such as "our customer data is not correct" or "the employee data is not consistent and integrated". Once issues have been identified they can be categorized and described clearly. The next step is to rank the issues based on how severe, frequent and detectable they are for example on a score from 1-5. Once scored we may start with the most important ones beginning to tackle them one-by-one. Prasanth suggest using a **fish-bone diagram** to become more knowledgeable about the cause-and-effects of these issues. It takes it's point of departure in a specific item on the list and considers what may cause this issue in terms of *measurements, materials, methods, environment, manpower* and *machines*. 

![[Fish bone diagram|center]]
Figure 5.1 Fish-bone diagram

The fish-bone diagram in figure 5.1 shows the example where an effect - inconsistent employee data has been identified. The causes may trace back to methods where there is no set procedure for filling in and creating records in the employee database. There may be multiple systems in place to remedy certain gaps so under machines we may write that HR systems that fulfill all needs are lacking. The result of this procedure may be that new policies are developed and communicated to the users that describe how data should be created in the system. It could also influence which IT projects are initiated, perhaps justifying investment in new IT-infrastructure. 

It's wellborn to aim for the highest data quality in every dimension, however it's typical to accept some *satisfactory* level. For example it could be that employee data is consistent 95% of the time or that the accuracy of IoT data sent is within 1 or 2 percentage points off. What is considered satisfactory depends on the context and the importance of the domains will likely also vary. In some critical compliance domains the completeness and accuracy of needs to be achieved 100% of the time. Other times it's not productive to spend time and resources needed to achieve 100% but any improvements in data quality should be welcomed, even if it's not possible to remove quality issues completely. Finally i want to reiterate that data quality management is a responsibility that is shared and should be a topic on the lips across the organization. 
## Data governance
The second leg of data management is data governance which was coined early on in the history of data management and as Laura describes it's an oversighting function that *“exercise authority and control (e.g., planning, monitoring, and enforcement) over the management of data assets.”*, (Sebastian-Coleman, page 62). An alternative definition can be found in *Practitioners guide to operationalizing data governance* by Mary Anne Hopper. Here data governance is defined as "*...the organizational framework establishing strategy, objectives, and policies for shared data assets*." (Hopper,  page 30). Both definitions indicate that data governance has an oversighting role in the larger data management program. 



Some of the original thoughts in the business management literature such as Henri Fayol's principles of management included principles like discipline and authority. His idea was that for organizations and workers to be effective, they must have some oversight and control which helps bring consistency, clarity and guidelines for what they must do. This is exactly what what data governance policies and procedures is there to do, bring consistency, clarity and guidelines for working with data. As the importance of data grows in organizations and their data and IT landscapes along with it that means more data sources which generate and produce data, more data to manage, more complexity in systems and greater cost of not achieving excellence with data. Governance suddenly shifts from being viewed as a fluffy and unnecessary discipline to one that is needed for worker productivity and more importantly long-term success with data as there are management which can step-back and view the bigger picture which individual teams and departments then do not need to think about. They can then just go about solving their problems most effectively guided by the frame in which they have to work.  When processes such as proposing new analytical requests or alerting about bugs are standardized, employees no longer have to think about those things and they can just get to the important work they need to do. Secondly when data architectures are deliberately designed and evolved, there will not be spider-web architectures and silo solutions where a lot of productivity and value is lost. Governance is also a shared responsibility as it ranges from very operational decisions such as naming conventions on infrastructure and data elements to the architectural and strategic thoughts about ETL processes and how use cases are defined. 

**Long-term sustainable governance of data**
Mary Anne Hopper has been involved in many data governance projects and must conclude that there are multiple challenges that needs to be addresses when it comes to succeeding with data governance. In her chapter *Rethinking data governance* she brings up some of these challenges that if not addresses, causes efforts to remain a academic exercise. A data governance program often introduces quite a lot of changes in procedures, behavior and thinking, something which requires a lot of work on the organization. Such work only really gets accepted and the necessary support if the people involved understand and can see clearly *why* this must happen. That is the business case of the data governance program should make it clear how it aligns with business strategies, such that it is obvious to the persons involved that lack of data governance is problematic and a root cause to some known issues. For example Hopper produces a long list problems stemming from ineffective data efforts she has met in her countless data governance projects. "*It costs too much to make simple change to a table or report*", "*We have to go to IT every time we want a new filter on our reports*", or "*we have no idea what data is available to us for use*". The policies and procedures of an oversighting program can be seen as strategies for addressing some of these concerns by creating consistency, clarity and guidelines. This could be finding ways to enable more self-service through education of users or building it in to the architecture. More examples of inspiration may be found in Mary lists on page 69. The second challenge is to recognize that managing data is not a standalone project. It's a discipline that to be *long-term sustainable* requires continuous sponsorship and commitment with visionary leaders. Mary points out how she may have left an organization very excited about the idea of data management, to come back months or years later and to her disappointment see the excitement has faded and everyone has fallen back into their old ways. The problem is that an organization needs to be aware that this is hard work which continues to be there. It may not seem to provide any immediate returns and that can discourage organizations. I am however of that belief that data success in the long-term (with emphasis on long) rests on the shoulders of good data governance. 

**A framework for putting data to productive use**
One the key claims of data management efforts is that it is there to increase the value of data. One of the ways that this shows up is through the development of a data strategy. A data strategy is a continuously evolving document and a deliberate effort to create alignment between the operating and business model and the role of data in these. Laura sums up this point "making decisions in the context of the overall business strategy makes more sense than making these decisions on a project-by-project basis" (Laura, page 80) A data strategy therefore seeks to create unity of direction focusing on the most important business cases. This is an exercise in high-level strategic thinking and exemplifies the diverse set of skills needed when practicing data management. A data strategy investigates and explore various ideas and put those in some order, such as a prioritization matrix. Data governance is not about bureaucracy but actually about productivity by exercising authority that removes obstacles and inconsistencies in day-to-day operations.

**Measuring data governance**
The oversighting activities of data governance are there to produce more long-term value with data. A data governance program needs a formal feedback loop which measures the success of this effort. Mary Anne Hopper usually asks her customers "Have you defined objectives?" and their answer is often no. This is a problem. It's difficult to gauge the effectiveness of any initiative if it's not strictly measured. That's for example why putting an electric meter in close proximity is creating more effective behavior to reduce consumption of electric energy. But how can we measure the success of the data governance program? For that we have to return to the why and the business case that justifies use of resources towards data governance. If it's to improve self-service capabilities, it could be to measure the number of users that have access to a shared data platform or follow-up surveys with stakeholders. It could be how much data is used which could be measured both using surveys or tracking usage of compute on various data assets. 
There can be many objectives that all relate back to the overall goal of data management, to obtain long-term sustainable success with data. 

There is a third major challenge which Hopper introduces. She suggest to make data governance measured since in it's idealized version it's a formal project with clear deliverables and most projects creates change management. , one that is often down prioritized in big project but unfortunately that means the motivation might be lost as a result which means all the investments are lost. I will admit that this is the point where i find that data management as described in frameworks like DAMA introduce a at times to long list of things to keep track of when it comes to data governance, at least unless it's the only discipline you practice. What i do really like  I will say it should revolve around making the work of people in the organization easier, removing some of the questions that arise so they can tackle things head on. We should build a business case for governance that people within the organization can understand and we could track a few key variables such as the time it takes to get new data sources integrated, number of days in handling bugs and so on. It takes time, it's hard work but the payoff is there.
